# Vocabulary & Grammar — CAT VARC Section

## What CAT Tests in Vocabulary & Grammar

CAT mein vocabulary aur grammar ke direct questions kam aate hain, but yeh skills INDIRECTLY poore VARC section mein kaam aati hain — RC passages samajhne mein, para jumbles solve karne mein, aur para summary mein correct option choose karne mein.

### Key point:
CAT does NOT test rare, obscure words. It tests whether you know the CORRECT USAGE of common English words. The difference between a 90th percentile and 99th percentile scorer is often just knowing when "affect" means something different from "effect."

## Part 1: Commonly Confused Word Pairs

These 50 word pairs are the ones most frequently tested or most relevant to CAT-level reading. Each pair includes the difference and an example showing correct usage.

### 1. Affect vs Effect
- **Affect** (verb) = to influence something
  - "The weather will **affect** the match."
- **Effect** (noun) = the result of something
  - "The **effect** of the policy was immediate."
- **Trick**: A for Action (Affect = verb = action), E for End result (Effect = noun)

### 2. Principle vs Principal
- **Principle** (noun) = a rule or fundamental truth
  - "She refused to compromise on her **principles**."
- **Principal** (noun/adj) = main/chief, or head of school
  - "The **principal** reason for the delay was funding."
- **Trick**: PrinciPAL = your PAL who is the head of school. PrinciPLE = a ruLE.

### 3. Complement vs Compliment
- **Complement** (noun/verb) = something that completes or goes well with
  - "The wine **complements** the cheese perfectly."
- **Compliment** (noun/verb) = praise
  - "She received a **compliment** on her presentation."
- **Trick**: Complement = complEte. Compliment = I like it (complIment).

### 4. Discrete vs Discreet
- **Discrete** (adj) = separate, distinct
  - "The data was divided into **discrete** categories."
- **Discreet** (adj) = careful, subtle, not attracting attention
  - "Please be **discreet** about this matter."
- **Trick**: The two E's in discrEEt are hiding together (being discreet).

### 5. Stationary vs Stationery
- **Stationary** (adj) = not moving
  - "The car was **stationary** at the traffic light."
- **Stationery** (noun) = writing materials (paper, pens, etc.)
  - "She bought new **stationery** for the office."
- **Trick**: StationEry = papEr. StationAry = stAying.

### 6. Eminent vs Imminent vs Immanent
- **Eminent** (adj) = famous, distinguished
  - "An **eminent** scientist addressed the conference."
- **Imminent** (adj) = about to happen very soon
  - "The storm is **imminent** — take shelter."
- **Immanent** (adj) = inherent, existing within
  - "The divine is **immanent** in all creation."

### 7. Allusion vs Illusion vs Delusion
- **Allusion** (noun) = an indirect reference
  - "The poem contains an **allusion** to Greek mythology."
- **Illusion** (noun) = a false perception or appearance
  - "The magician created an **illusion** of disappearing."
- **Delusion** (noun) = a false belief held despite evidence
  - "He suffers from the **delusion** that he is always right."

### 8. Imply vs Infer
- **Imply** (verb) = to suggest without directly saying (speaker implies)
  - "Are you **implying** that I'm wrong?"
- **Infer** (verb) = to conclude from evidence (listener infers)
  - "From the data, we can **infer** that sales are declining."
- **Trick**: The speaker IMPLIES, the listener INFERS. I-M-ply = I-M sending the message. I-N-fer = I-N-take the message.

### 9. Elicit vs Illicit
- **Elicit** (verb) = to draw out, to bring forth
  - "The question **elicited** a strong response."
- **Illicit** (adj) = illegal, unlawful
  - "The police discovered **illicit** drugs."

### 10. Emigrate vs Immigrate
- **Emigrate** = to LEAVE a country
  - "She **emigrated** from India to Canada."
- **Immigrate** = to ENTER a country
  - "She **immigrated** to Canada from India."
- **Trick**: Emigrate = Exit. Immigrate = Into.

### 11. Farther vs Further
- **Farther** = greater physical distance
  - "The airport is **farther** than I thought."
- **Further** = additional, to a greater degree (abstract)
  - "Let's discuss this **further** tomorrow."

### 12. Fewer vs Less
- **Fewer** = countable things
  - "There are **fewer** students this year."
- **Less** = uncountable things
  - "There is **less** water in the tank."
- **Trick**: If you can count it, use fewer. If you can't, use less.

### 13. Comprise vs Compose
- **Comprise** = the whole COMPRISES the parts (no "of")
  - "The committee **comprises** ten members." (CORRECT)
  - "The committee is comprised of ten members." (INCORRECT — avoid this)
- **Compose** = the parts COMPOSE the whole
  - "Ten members **compose** the committee."
- **Trick**: The whole COMPRISES the parts. The parts COMPOSE the whole.

### 14. Continual vs Continuous
- **Continual** = repeated but with interruptions
  - "The **continual** interruptions disrupted the meeting."
- **Continuous** = unbroken, without stopping
  - "The machine runs **continuously** for 24 hours."

### 15. Credible vs Credulous
- **Credible** = believable
  - "Her testimony was **credible** and convincing."
- **Credulous** = too willing to believe (gullible)
  - "**Credulous** investors fell for the scam."

### 16. Disinterested vs Uninterested
- **Disinterested** = impartial, unbiased
  - "A judge must be **disinterested** in the case."
- **Uninterested** = not interested, bored
  - "The students looked **uninterested** during the lecture."
- **Important**: This is frequently tested. "Disinterested" does NOT mean "bored."

### 17. Flout vs Flaunt
- **Flout** = to openly disregard a rule
  - "He **flouted** the speed limit regularly."
- **Flaunt** = to show off
  - "She **flaunted** her new jewelry."

### 18. Hoard vs Horde
- **Hoard** (noun/verb) = a stockpile / to stockpile
  - "He **hoarded** supplies during the shortage."
- **Horde** (noun) = a large, unruly group
  - "A **horde** of fans rushed the stage."

### 19. Liable vs Libel
- **Liable** = legally responsible, or likely to
  - "The company is **liable** for damages."
- **Libel** = written defamation
  - "The newspaper faced a **libel** lawsuit."

### 20. Loath vs Loathe
- **Loath** (adj) = unwilling, reluctant
  - "She was **loath** to admit her mistake."
- **Loathe** (verb) = to hate intensely
  - "I **loathe** dishonesty."

### 21. Mitigate vs Militate
- **Mitigate** = to make less severe
  - "The medicine will **mitigate** the pain."
- **Militate** = to work against
  - "Several factors **militate** against success."

### 22. Precede vs Proceed
- **Precede** = to come before
  - "Thunder is usually **preceded** by lightning."
- **Proceed** = to continue, to go forward
  - "Please **proceed** to the next question."

### 23. Prescribe vs Proscribe
- **Prescribe** = to recommend or order (medicine, rules)
  - "The doctor **prescribed** antibiotics."
- **Proscribe** = to forbid, to ban
  - "The law **proscribes** discrimination."

### 24. Ambiguous vs Ambivalent
- **Ambiguous** = having multiple meanings, unclear
  - "The contract language was **ambiguous**."
- **Ambivalent** = having mixed feelings
  - "She felt **ambivalent** about the promotion."

### 25. Amoral vs Immoral
- **Amoral** = having no moral sense, not caring about right/wrong
  - "Animals are **amoral** — they act on instinct."
- **Immoral** = violating moral principles
  - "Cheating on exams is **immoral**."

### 26. Averse vs Adverse
- **Averse** = opposed to, having a strong dislike
  - "She is **averse** to taking risks."
- **Adverse** = harmful, unfavorable
  - "The drug had **adverse** side effects."

### 27. Censor vs Censure
- **Censor** = to suppress or remove objectionable content
  - "The government **censored** the newspaper article."
- **Censure** = to express strong disapproval
  - "The committee **censured** the member for misconduct."

### 28. Climatic vs Climactic
- **Climatic** = relating to climate
  - "**Climatic** changes are affecting agriculture."
- **Climactic** = relating to a climax (most exciting point)
  - "The **climactic** scene of the movie was breathtaking."

### 29. Complacent vs Complaisant
- **Complacent** = self-satisfied, smug
  - "Don't become **complacent** after one success."
- **Complaisant** = willing to please others
  - "The **complaisant** host agreed to every request."

### 30. Defuse vs Diffuse
- **Defuse** = to reduce tension, to make safe
  - "The negotiator **defused** the crisis."
- **Diffuse** (verb) = to spread out; (adj) = spread widely
  - "The scent **diffused** throughout the room."

### 31. Deprecate vs Depreciate
- **Deprecate** = to express disapproval of
  - "He **deprecated** the use of violence."
- **Depreciate** = to decrease in value
  - "Cars **depreciate** quickly in the first year."

### 32. Efficacy vs Efficiency
- **Efficacy** = the ability to produce a desired result
  - "The **efficacy** of the vaccine was 95%."
- **Efficiency** = achieving maximum output with minimum wasted effort
  - "The new system improved **efficiency** by 30%."

### 33. Endemic vs Epidemic vs Pandemic
- **Endemic** = regularly found in a particular area
  - "Malaria is **endemic** to tropical regions."
- **Epidemic** = widespread outbreak in a community/region
  - "The flu **epidemic** affected thousands."
- **Pandemic** = worldwide outbreak
  - "COVID-19 was declared a **pandemic** in 2020."

### 34. Exigent vs Exiguous
- **Exigent** = urgent, demanding immediate action
  - "The **exigent** circumstances required quick decisions."
- **Exiguous** = very small, meager
  - "He survived on **exiguous** rations."

### 35. Fortuitous vs Fortunate
- **Fortuitous** = happening by chance (not necessarily good)
  - "Their meeting was entirely **fortuitous**."
- **Fortunate** = lucky, favorable
  - "We were **fortunate** to escape the storm."

### 36. Ingenious vs Ingenuous
- **Ingenious** = clever, inventive
  - "She found an **ingenious** solution to the problem."
- **Ingenuous** = innocent, naive, candid
  - "His **ingenuous** manner charmed everyone."

### 37. Luxuriant vs Luxurious
- **Luxuriant** = growing abundantly, lush
  - "The **luxuriant** vegetation covered the hillside."
- **Luxurious** = very comfortable, expensive
  - "They stayed in a **luxurious** hotel."

### 38. Officious vs Official
- **Officious** = asserting authority in an annoying way
  - "The **officious** clerk demanded unnecessary paperwork."
- **Official** = authorized, formal
  - "The **official** statement was released at noon."

### 39. Perpetrate vs Perpetuate
- **Perpetrate** = to commit (a crime or wrongful act)
  - "He **perpetrated** a massive fraud."
- **Perpetuate** = to cause to continue indefinitely
  - "These stereotypes **perpetuate** discrimination."

### 40. Pragmatic vs Dogmatic
- **Pragmatic** = practical, dealing with things realistically
  - "She took a **pragmatic** approach to the budget."
- **Dogmatic** = rigidly following beliefs, not open to discussion
  - "His **dogmatic** views left no room for compromise."

### 41. Reticent vs Reluctant
- **Reticent** = reserved, not revealing one's thoughts
  - "She was **reticent** about her personal life."
- **Reluctant** = unwilling
  - "He was **reluctant** to take the assignment."
- **Note**: Reticent is specifically about NOT SPEAKING. Reluctant is about NOT WANTING to do something.

### 42. Tortuous vs Torturous
- **Tortuous** = winding, complex
  - "The **tortuous** mountain road was difficult to navigate."
- **Torturous** = causing torture, extremely painful
  - "The **torturous** wait for results lasted three weeks."

### 43. Turbid vs Turgid
- **Turbid** = cloudy, muddy (liquids)
  - "The river was **turbid** after the rains."
- **Turgid** = swollen; also, pompously complex (writing)
  - "His **turgid** prose was almost unreadable."

### 44. Venal vs Venial
- **Venal** = corrupt, willing to act dishonestly for money
  - "The **venal** politician accepted bribes."
- **Venial** = minor, forgivable (of a sin or fault)
  - "It was a **venial** error, easily corrected."

### 45. Wreak vs Reek
- **Wreak** = to cause damage or havoc
  - "The storm **wreaked** havoc on the coast."
- **Reek** = to smell strongly and unpleasantly
  - "The alley **reeked** of garbage."

### 46. Appraise vs Apprise
- **Appraise** = to assess the value of
  - "The jeweler **appraised** the diamond."
- **Apprise** = to inform
  - "Please **apprise** me of any changes."

### 47. Assent vs Ascent
- **Assent** = agreement
  - "The board gave its **assent** to the proposal."
- **Ascent** = upward movement
  - "The **ascent** of the mountain took three days."

### 48. Banal vs Venal
- **Banal** = boring, unoriginal
  - "The movie's plot was predictable and **banal**."
- **Venal** = corrupt (see #44 above)

### 49. Equitable vs Equable
- **Equitable** = fair, just
  - "The resources were distributed in an **equitable** manner."
- **Equable** = calm, even-tempered; or uniform
  - "She maintained an **equable** temperament despite the stress."

### 50. Palpable vs Palatable
- **Palpable** = able to be felt or touched; obvious
  - "The tension in the room was **palpable**."
- **Palatable** = pleasant to taste; acceptable
  - "The proposal needs to be made more **palatable** to voters."

## Part 2: Important Idioms and Phrases

These 30 idioms and phrases frequently appear in CAT RC passages and questions. Knowing them helps you understand the passage better and answer vocabulary-in-context questions.

### 1. Achilles' heel
**Meaning**: A weakness in spite of overall strength
**Example**: "His inability to delegate was his **Achilles' heel** as a manager."

### 2. At the drop of a hat
**Meaning**: Without hesitation, immediately
**Example**: "She would argue **at the drop of a hat**."

### 3. Beat around the bush
**Meaning**: Avoid getting to the main point
**Example**: "Stop **beating around the bush** and tell me what happened."

### 4. Between a rock and a hard place
**Meaning**: Faced with two equally undesirable options
**Example**: "The government is caught **between a rock and a hard place** on the policy."

### 5. Bite the bullet
**Meaning**: To endure a painful situation with courage
**Example**: "We need to **bite the bullet** and make the cuts."

### 6. By and large
**Meaning**: On the whole, generally
**Example**: "**By and large**, the policy has been successful."

### 7. Call into question
**Meaning**: To cause doubt about something
**Example**: "The new evidence **calls into question** the earlier findings."

### 8. Carte blanche
**Meaning**: Complete freedom to act as one wishes
**Example**: "The CEO gave him **carte blanche** to restructure the department."

### 9. Catch-22
**Meaning**: A dilemma where the solution to one problem creates another
**Example**: "It's a **Catch-22**: you need experience to get a job, but you need a job to get experience."

### 10. Cut corners
**Meaning**: To do something in the easiest or cheapest way, reducing quality
**Example**: "Safety was compromised because the contractor **cut corners**."

### 11. Devil's advocate
**Meaning**: One who argues the opposing side for the sake of argument
**Example**: "Let me play **devil's advocate** — what if the plan fails?"

### 12. Double-edged sword
**Meaning**: Something that has both positive and negative consequences
**Example**: "Social media is a **double-edged sword** for teenagers."

### 13. Fait accompli
**Meaning**: Something already done and irreversible

### 14. Give short shrift
**Meaning**: To treat something as unimportant

### 15. In lieu of
**Meaning**: Instead of
**Example**: "He accepted stock options **in lieu of** a salary increase."

### 16. Moot point
**Meaning**: A debatable or irrelevant issue
**Example**: "Whether to expand is now a **moot point** — the budget has been cut."

### 17. Modus operandi
**Meaning**: A particular way of doing things
**Example**: "Secrecy was the organization's **modus operandi**."

### 18. On the fence
**Meaning**: Undecided
**Example**: "Many voters are still **on the fence** about the candidate."

### 19. Par for the course
**Meaning**: Normal, to be expected
**Example**: "Delays are **par for the course** in government projects."

### 20. Play devil's advocate
**Meaning**: To argue the opposite side to test the strength of an argument
**Example**: "I'll **play devil's advocate** here — what about the cost?"

### 21. Quid pro quo
**Meaning**: Something given in return for something else
**Example**: "The deal was a **quid pro quo** — votes for contracts."

### 22. Red herring
**Meaning**: Something misleading or distracting from the main issue
**Example**: "The accusation was a **red herring** to divert attention from the real scandal."

### 23. Sine qua non
**Meaning**: An essential condition, something absolutely necessary
**Example**: "Trust is the **sine qua non** of any successful partnership."

### 24. Status quo
**Meaning**: The existing state of affairs
**Example**: "The party promised to maintain the **status quo** on tax policy."

### 25. Straw man
**Meaning**: A weak argument set up to be easily defeated
**Example**: "He created a **straw man** by misrepresenting his opponent's position."

### 26. The crux of the matter
**Meaning**: The most important point
**Example**: "**The crux of the matter** is whether we can afford it."

### 27. Throw the baby out with the bathwater
**Meaning**: To discard something valuable along with something undesirable
**Example**: "Reforming the system doesn't mean we should **throw the baby out with the bathwater**."

### 28. Turn a blind eye
**Meaning**: To deliberately ignore something

### 29. Vis-a-vis

### 30. White elephant
**Meaning**: A costly possession that is a burden to maintain

## Part 3: Grammar Concepts Tested in CAT

CAT doesn't have explicit "grammar" questions, but grammar knowledge is essential for:
- Sentence Correction (if asked)
- Understanding RC passages correctly
- Para Jumbles (identifying correct flow)
- Para Summary (identifying correct paraphrasing)

### Concept 1: Subject-Verb Agreement

**Rule**: The subject and verb must agree in number (singular/plural).

**Common traps**:

**Trap 1 — Intervening phrases**:
The subject and verb are separated by a phrase.
- WRONG: "The quality of the products **are** excellent."
- RIGHT: "The quality of the products **is** excellent."
- Why: Subject = "quality" (singular), not "products." "Of the products" is just a prepositional phrase.

**Trap 2 — "Either...or" and "Neither...nor"**:
The verb agrees with the subject CLOSEST to it.
- "Neither the manager nor the employees **were** informed." (employees = plural → were)
- "Neither the employees nor the manager **was** informed." (manager = singular → was)

**Trap 3 — Collective nouns**:
Words like "team," "committee," "jury," "government" can be singular or plural.
- "The committee **has** made its decision." (acting as one unit)
- "The committee **have** disagreed among themselves." (acting as individuals — British English)
- In CAT, treat collective nouns as SINGULAR unless clearly acting individually.

**Trap 4 — "Each," "Every," "Everyone," "Nobody"**:
These are ALWAYS singular.
- "Each of the students **has** submitted the assignment." (not "have")
- "Everyone **is** expected to attend." (not "are")

**Trap 5 — Inverted sentences**:
When the sentence structure is inverted, identify the REAL subject.
- "In the box **were** several documents." (Subject = "documents" = plural → "were")
- "There **is** a book on the table." (Subject = "book" = singular → "is")

### Concept 2: Pronoun Reference Errors

**Rule**: Every pronoun must clearly refer to one specific noun (antecedent).

**Common errors**:

**Ambiguous reference**:
- WRONG: "When John met Peter, **he** was nervous." (Who was nervous? John or Peter?)
- RIGHT: "When John met Peter, **John** was nervous." OR "John was nervous when he met Peter."

**No antecedent**:
- WRONG: "In the report, **they** recommend more funding." (Who are "they"?)
- RIGHT: "In the report, **the researchers** recommend more funding."

**Pronoun-antecedent number mismatch**:
- WRONG: "Each student must submit **their** assignment." (Each = singular, their = plural)
- RIGHT: "Each student must submit **his or her** assignment." (formal)
- Also acceptable: "All students must submit **their** assignments." (rewrite to avoid the issue)

### Concept 3: Parallel Structure

**Rule**: Items in a list or comparison must be in the same grammatical form.

**Examples**:

**Lists**:
- WRONG: "She likes **reading**, **to swim**, and **jogging**."
- RIGHT: "She likes **reading**, **swimming**, and **jogging**." (all gerunds)
- Also RIGHT: "She likes **to read**, **to swim**, and **to jog**." (all infinitives)

**Comparisons**:
- WRONG: "Working in an office is different from **to work** at home."
- RIGHT: "Working in an office is different from **working** at home."

**Correlative conjunctions** (both...and, either...or, neither...nor, not only...but also):
- WRONG: "He is **not only** intelligent **but also** has charm."
- RIGHT: "He is **not only** intelligent **but also** charming." (both adjectives)
- RIGHT: "He **not only** has intelligence **but also** has charm." (both verb phrases)

### Concept 4: Dangling Modifiers

**Rule**: A modifying phrase at the beginning of a sentence must refer to the subject that immediately follows it.

**Examples**:
- WRONG: "**Walking through the park**, the flowers were beautiful."
  - (Who was walking? The flowers? That doesn't make sense.)
- RIGHT: "**Walking through the park**, **I** noticed the beautiful flowers."
  - (Now "I" is walking through the park — makes sense.)

- WRONG: "**Having studied all night**, the exam was easy."
  - (The exam didn't study all night!)
- RIGHT: "**Having studied all night**, **she** found the exam easy."

**How to check**: Read the opening phrase and the subject together. Does it make logical sense? "Walking through the park, I..." — yes. "Walking through the park, the flowers..." — no.

### Concept 5: Tense Consistency

**Rule**: Maintain consistent verb tense within a sentence/paragraph unless there's a clear reason to change.

**Examples**:
- WRONG: "He **walked** into the room and **sits** down." (past → present)
- RIGHT: "He **walked** into the room and **sat** down." (both past)

- WRONG: "The report **showed** that the economy **is** growing." (past → present)
- RIGHT: "The report **showed** that the economy **was** growing." (both past)
- EXCEPTION: "The report showed that the earth **revolves** around the sun." (General truth = present tense is OK even in past context)

### Concept 6: Articles (A, An, The)

**Rules**:

**"A" vs "An"**:
- "An" before a vowel SOUND (not vowel letter)
- "**An** hour" (h is silent, starts with vowel sound)
- "**A** university" (u sounds like "yoo" = consonant sound)
- "**An** MBA" (M = "em" = vowel sound)
- "**A** one-time offer" (one starts with "w" sound)

**"The" (definite article)**:
- Use "the" when referring to something SPECIFIC or already mentioned
  - "**The** book you recommended was excellent."
- Use "the" with superlatives: "**the** tallest building"
- Use "the" with unique things: "**the** sun," "**the** earth," "**the** internet"
- DO NOT use "the" with general plurals: "**Dogs** are loyal" (not "The dogs are loyal" when talking about dogs in general)

**No article**:
- Before uncountable nouns used generally: "**Water** is essential." (not "The water")
- Before most proper nouns: "**India** is diverse." (not "The India")
- EXCEPTIONS with "the": The United States, The Maldives, The Himalayas (plural/collective names)

## Part 4: Sentence Correction Approach

When you encounter a sentence that might have an error, follow this systematic 6-step check:

### Step 1: Read the sentence — does it SOUND wrong?

Read it naturally. If something feels off, it probably is. Trust your ear, but verify with rules.

### Step 2: Check Subject-Verb Agreement

- Find the main subject
- Find the main verb
- Do they agree in number?
- Watch for intervening phrases that try to distract you

### Step 3: Check Pronoun Reference

- Find every pronoun (he, she, it, they, this, these, which, who)
- Does each pronoun have a clear antecedent?
- Do they agree in number (singular pronoun for singular noun)?

### Step 4: Check Parallel Structure

- Find any lists, comparisons, or correlative conjunctions
- Are all items in the same grammatical form?

### Step 5: Check Modifier Placement

- Is there a phrase at the beginning of the sentence?
- Does it logically modify the subject that follows it?
- Are adjectives and adverbs near the words they modify?

### Step 6: Check Idiom Usage

- Are common expressions used correctly?
- "Different FROM" (not "different than")
- "Regard AS" (not "regard to be")
- "Prohibit FROM" (not "prohibit to")
- "Not only X but also Y" (not "Not only X and also Y")

## Practice: Sentence Correction

### Q1. Identify and correct the error:

"The data collected from the survey **suggests** that most employees **prefers** flexible working hours."

**Answer**: "prefers" should be "prefer"
**Explanation**: "Most employees" = plural subject → needs plural verb "prefer." ("Data suggests" is acceptable — "data" is often treated as singular in modern usage.)

### Q2. Identify and correct the error:

"**Having completed** the project ahead of schedule, the client **was impressed** by the team's efficiency."

**Answer**: Dangling modifier. "Having completed the project" modifies "the client" — but the CLIENT didn't complete the project.
**Correct**: "Having completed the project ahead of schedule, **the team** impressed the client with **its** efficiency."

### Q3. Identify and correct the error:

"Neither the CEO nor the board members **was** aware of the financial irregularities."

**Answer**: "was" should be "were"
**Explanation**: "Neither...nor" — verb agrees with the CLOSER subject. "Board members" (plural) is closer → "were."

### Q4. Identify and correct the error:

"The company's strategy involves reducing costs, **to increase** revenue, and expanding into new markets."

**Answer**: "to increase" should be "increasing"
**Explanation**: Parallel structure. The list should be: "reducing, increasing, expanding" (all gerunds). "To increase" breaks the pattern.

### Q5. Identify and correct the error:

"Each of the candidates **have** submitted **their** applications before the deadline."

**Answer**: Two errors. "have" should be "has" (Each = singular). "Their" should be "his or her" (to agree with singular "each").
**Correct**: "Each of the candidates **has** submitted **his or her** application before the deadline."

### Q6. Identify and correct the error:

"The reason for the delay **is because** the supplier failed to deliver materials on time."

**Answer**: "is because" is redundant. "Reason" already implies "because."
**Correct**: "The reason for the delay **is that** the supplier failed to deliver materials on time."
**Also correct**: "The delay occurred **because** the supplier failed to deliver materials on time."

### Q7. Identify and correct the error:

"Not only **the employees protested** the new policy, but also **the management expressed** concerns."

**Answer**: Incorrect placement with "not only...but also."
**Correct**: "**Not only did the employees protest** the new policy, **but the management also expressed** concerns."
**Why**: "Not only" at the start of a clause triggers subject-verb inversion (did the employees protest).

### Q8. Identify and correct the error:

"The government has announced that it will distribute **less** textbooks to schools this year due to budget constraints."

**Answer**: "less" should be "fewer"
**Explanation**: "Textbooks" are countable → use "fewer." "Less" is for uncountable nouns (less money, less water).

### Q9. Identify and correct the error:

"The committee discussed the proposal and **have** decided to implement it in **an** phased manner."

**Answer**: Two errors. "Have" should be "has" (committee = singular collective noun). "An" should be "a" (phased starts with a consonant sound "f").
**Correct**: "The committee discussed the proposal and **has** decided to implement it in **a** phased manner."

### Q10. Identify and correct the error:

"**Running** through the forest, **a branch** scratched her face."

**Answer**: Dangling modifier. "Running through the forest" modifies "a branch" — the branch wasn't running.
**Correct**: "**Running** through the forest, **she** was scratched by a branch."
**Also correct**: "**As she ran** through the forest, a branch scratched her face."

### Q11. Identify and correct the error:

"The new policy affects everyone, **irregardless** of their position in the company."

**Answer**: "irregardless" is not standard English. Use "regardless."
**Correct**: "The new policy affects everyone, **regardless** of their position in the company."

### Q12. Identify and correct the error:

"She is one of those managers who **believes** in empowering her team members."

**Answer**: "believes" should be "believe"
**Explanation**: "Who" refers to "managers" (plural), not "one." The clause is "managers who believe in empowering..." So the verb must agree with the plural antecedent "managers."
**Correct**: "She is one of those managers who **believe** in empowering their team members."

### Q13. Identify and correct the error:

"The professor, along with his students, **were** conducting research in the laboratory."

**Answer**: "were" should be "was"
**Explanation**: "Along with his students" is an intervening phrase, NOT part of the subject. The subject is "The professor" (singular) → "was."
**Rule**: Phrases like "along with," "together with," "as well as," "in addition to" do NOT make the subject plural.

### Q14. Identify and correct the error:

"He insisted on me **going** to the meeting instead of him."

**Answer**: "me going" should be "my going"
**Explanation**: Before a gerund (going), use the possessive form (my, his, her, their), not the objective form (me, him, her, them).
**Correct**: "He insisted on **my** going to the meeting instead of him."

### Q15. Identify and correct the error:

"The population of Tokyo is greater than **Delhi**."

**Answer**: Faulty comparison. You're comparing Tokyo's POPULATION with the CITY of Delhi.
**Correct**: "The population of Tokyo is greater than **that of** Delhi."
**Rule**: Compare like with like — population with population, not population with city.

### Q16. Identify and correct the error:

"I would have went to the concert if I had known about it earlier."

**Answer**: "would have went" should be "would have gone"
**Explanation**: After "would have" (or any have/has/had), use the PAST PARTICIPLE, not the simple past. Go → went (simple past) → gone (past participle).

### Q17. Identify and correct the error:

"The book, which I bought yesterday from the bookstore near my office, and which I started reading on the way home, **were** very interesting."

**Answer**: "were" should be "was"
**Explanation**: Subject = "The book" (singular). The two "which" clauses are just additional information (parenthetical). The verb must agree with "book" → "was."

### Q18. Identify and correct the error:

"Between the three candidates, she was the most qualified."

**Answer**: "Between" should be "Among"
**Explanation**: "Between" is used for TWO items. "Among" is used for THREE or more.
**Correct**: "**Among** the three candidates, she was the most qualified."

### Q19. Identify and correct the error:

"If I **was** the prime minister, I would reform the education system."

**Answer**: "was" should be "were"
**Explanation**: This is a HYPOTHETICAL/SUBJUNCTIVE sentence (If I were...). For unreal conditions, use "were" regardless of the subject.
**Correct**: "If I **were** the prime minister, I would reform the education system."

### Q20. Identify and correct the error:

"The company's profits this year are **more higher** than last year."

**Answer**: "more higher" is a double comparative. Use either "higher" or "more high" (but "higher" is standard).
**Correct**: "The company's profits this year are **higher** than last year."
**Rule**: Never use "more" with comparative forms (-er). More better, more faster, more higher — all wrong.

## Quick Reference: Grammar Error Detection Priorities

When scanning for errors, check in this order (most common first):

| Priority | Error Type | How to Spot |
|----------|-----------|-------------|
| 1 | Subject-verb agreement | Find subject, find verb, check if they match |
| 2 | Pronoun errors | Find pronouns, check antecedents + number agreement |
| 3 | Parallel structure | Find lists/comparisons, check consistent form |
| 4 | Dangling modifiers | Check opening phrases against the subject |
| 5 | Tense consistency | Read through for tense shifts |
| 6 | Articles | Check a/an based on SOUND, the vs no article |
| 7 | Word usage | Check commonly confused words |
| 8 | Idiom correctness | Verify standard expressions |
| 9 | Comparisons | Like compared with like? |
| 10 | Double negatives/comparatives | "More better," "can't hardly" |

## Key Takeaways

1. **CAT tests USAGE, not vocabulary size** — know how to USE common words correctly
2. **Learn the 50 confused pairs** — these come up in RC passages and answer options constantly
3. **Subject-verb agreement is the #1 grammar trap** — look for intervening phrases
4. **Parallel structure errors are very common** — always check lists and comparisons
5. **Dangling modifiers appear in almost every CAT** — the opening phrase must modify the subject
6. **"Less" vs "fewer" is a classic CAT trap** — countable = fewer, uncountable = less
7. **Read quality English daily** — The Hindu, Economist, Aeon — this builds intuitive grammar
8. **When in doubt, SIMPLIFY** — rephrase the sentence in your head. The simpler version usually reveals the error.
9. **Practice 10 sentence corrections daily** — takes only 15-20 minutes, builds strong grammar instincts
10. **Don't memorize rules in isolation** — practice spotting errors in CONTEXT (real sentences, passages)
