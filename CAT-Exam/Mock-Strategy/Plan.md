## 1. Why Mocks Matter More Than Anything

### The Hard Truth

Most students spend 90% of their time studying concepts and only 10% on mocks. The toppers do the opposite — they spend at least 50% of their time on mocks and mock analysis.

Here is why:

**Theory knowledge is NOT equal to exam score.**

Your CAT score is a combination of FOUR things:
1. **Knowledge** — Do you know the concepts? (This is what studying gives you)
2. **Time management** — Can you solve under pressure in 40 minutes per section?
3. **Accuracy** — Can you avoid silly mistakes when the clock is ticking?
4. **Stamina** — Can you stay sharp for 2 full hours without your brain getting tired?

Studying only gives you #1. Mocks give you ALL FOUR.

### The Numbers Don't Lie

Research across coaching institutes shows:

| Mocks Taken | Average Percentile Improvement |
|-------------|-------------------------------|
| Less than 10 mocks | Baseline (no improvement from mocks) |
| 10-20 mocks | +5 to +10 percentile |
| 20-30 mocks | +10 to +15 percentile |
| 30+ mocks | +15 to +20 percentile |

Students who take 30+ mocks consistently score 15-20 percentile HIGHER than students with the same level of concept knowledge who take fewer than 10 mocks.

### Think About It This Way

Imagine two students:
- **Student A**: Knows every formula, every shortcut, every concept. Has taken 5 mocks.
- **Student B**: Knows 80% of concepts but has taken 35 mocks with deep analysis.

Student B will DESTROY Student A on exam day. Why?

Because Student B:
- Knows exactly how to manage 40 minutes per section
- Has seen every type of question pattern CAT throws
- Has made (and fixed) every silly mistake already
- Can stay calm under pressure because they have done this 35 times before
- Knows which questions to skip and which to attempt
- Has built exam-day stamina

Student A will panic, waste time on hard questions, make silly mistakes, and run out of time.

### The Bottom Line

**You cannot "study" your way to a 99 percentile. You must "mock" your way there.**

## 2. Mock Schedule — When and How Often

### The Golden Rule: Start Early

**Start taking mocks AT LEAST 3 months before CAT.** Don't wait until you "finish the syllabus" — you will never feel 100% ready. That is normal. Take mocks anyway.

Even if you have only covered 50% of the syllabus, take a mock. You will:
- See the exam pattern
- Understand question difficulty
- Identify your natural strengths and weaknesses
- Build exam stamina from day one

### Recommended Schedule

#### Phase 1: Foundation Phase (3-4 months before CAT)
- **1 mock per week** (Saturdays)
- Focus: Getting used to the format, building stamina
- Don't worry about scores — focus on completing the mock and doing analysis
- Continue studying concepts on other days

#### Phase 2: Acceleration Phase (2-3 months before CAT)
- **1 mock per week** (Saturdays) + **1 sectional mock midweek** (Wednesday)
- Focus: Improving time management, fixing weak areas found in analysis
- Start tracking scores in your spreadsheet
- Target: Steady improvement, not perfection

#### Phase 3: Intensive Phase (Last 1 month before CAT)
- **2 full mocks per week** (Saturday + Wednesday)
- Focus: Fine-tuning strategy, building consistency
- No new topics — only revision and mock analysis
- Every mock should simulate real exam conditions perfectly

### Weekly Mock Routine

| Day | Activity |
|-----|----------|
| Monday | Revise weak topics from last mock analysis |
| Tuesday | Practice questions from weak areas |
| Wednesday | Sectional mock OR full mock (Phase 2/3) |
| Thursday | Analyze Wednesday mock + concept revision |
| Friday | Light practice + review mistake log + rest |
| Saturday AM | FULL MOCK (2 hours, exam conditions) |
| Saturday PM | MOCK ANALYSIS (1-2 hours) |
| Sunday | Rest + light revision + plan next week |

### Important Timing Note

**Saturday morning = full mock, follow exact exam timing.**

CAT is held in specific slots (morning or afternoon). Find out your slot and take your mocks at the SAME TIME. If your CAT slot is 8:30 AM, take all your mocks at 8:30 AM. Train your brain to peak at that time.

## 4. How to Take a Mock (Simulate Real Exam)

### The Number One Rule: Make It REAL

The biggest mistake students make is taking mocks casually — on the phone, in bed, with the TV on, pausing to check WhatsApp. This is a COMPLETE WASTE OF TIME.

If your mock conditions don't match real exam conditions, your mock score is meaningless.

### Pre-Mock Setup Checklist

Before every mock, prepare:

- [ ] **Quiet room** — Close the door. Tell family you are unavailable for 2 hours.
- [ ] **Computer or laptop** — Never take a mock on your phone. CAT is on a computer.
- [ ] **Full battery or plugged in** — Don't let your laptop die mid-mock.
- [ ] **Timer set for exactly 2 hours** — Use a separate timer if the mock platform doesn't have one.
- [ ] **Phone on silent and in another room** — Not on the desk. Not on vibrate. In ANOTHER ROOM.
- [ ] **No calculator** — CAT does not allow calculators. Do all math on paper.
- [ ] **Rough sheets ready** — Keep 5-6 blank sheets and 2 pens on the desk.
- [ ] **Water bottle on desk** — Stay hydrated but don't leave to get water.
- [ ] **Bathroom visit before starting** — Don't waste mock time.
- [ ] **No food on desk** — Eat before the mock, not during.

### During the Mock

- **Do NOT pause** — In the real exam, you cannot pause. Treat the mock the same way.
- **Do NOT look up answers** — If you don't know a question, skip it and move on, just like in the real exam.
- **Do NOT go back to previous sections** — CAT is sectional. Once 40 minutes for a section are up, you move to the next section. You CANNOT go back. (Note: Most mock platforms enforce this automatically.)
- **Do NOT get up** — Stay seated for the full 2 hours.
- **Use the on-screen calculator only if the mock platform provides one** — Some mock platforms give you a basic on-screen calculator (like CAT does). Use it if available, but don't bring a physical calculator.

### After the Mock

- **Don't look at solutions immediately.** Take a 15-30 minute break first.
- Walk around. Drink water. Clear your head.
- THEN come back for analysis (see Section 7 below — this is the most important part).

## 5. Time Management Strategy Per Section

CAT has 3 sections, each with 40 minutes. You CANNOT move between sections. Once a section's time is up, it is locked.

This means time management WITHIN each section is critical. Here is the strategy:

---

### VARC — Verbal Ability and Reading Comprehension (40 minutes)

**Section structure:**
- Usually 4-5 RC (Reading Comprehension) passages with 3-5 questions each
- 5-6 VA (Verbal Ability) questions: para jumble, odd sentence out, para summary

**Time allocation:**

| Activity | Time | Notes |
|----------|------|-------|
| Scan all passages (read first 2 lines of each) | 2-3 min | Identify easy vs hard passages |
| Attempt 3 RC passages | ~30 min (~10 min each) | Choose the 3 easiest |
| VA questions (para jumble, odd one out, summary) | ~8-10 min | These are quick once you know the tricks |

**Strategy:**
1. **First 2-3 minutes: SCAN.** Read the first 2 lines of every RC passage. Mark them mentally as Easy, Medium, or Hard based on:
   - Topic familiarity (familiar topic = easier for you)
   - Passage length (shorter = faster)
   - Language complexity (simple English = easier)
   - Question types (factual questions are easier than inference questions)

2. **Attempt 3 RC passages (skip the hardest 1-2).** This is the key to VARC success. Many students try to do all passages and end up rushing everything. Do 3 passages well instead of 5 passages badly.

3. **For each RC passage:**
   - Read the passage carefully ONCE (3-4 minutes)
   - Don't re-read the whole passage — just refer back for specific details
   - Answer questions in order
   - If stuck on one question for >1 minute, mark it and move on

4. **VA questions last (or first if you are strong in VA).** Para jumble and odd sentence out can be done quickly (1-2 minutes each) if you know the technique. Para summary takes 2-3 minutes.

**Targets:**
- Attempts: 18-20 questions
- Accuracy: 75-80%
- Time per RC passage: 8-10 minutes (including reading + questions)

**Golden Rules for VARC:**
- Never spend more than 10 minutes on ONE passage
- If a passage is boring or confusing after reading half of it, SKIP IT
- For RC, the answer is ALWAYS in the passage — don't use outside knowledge
- For para jumble, look for the opening sentence first (usually one sentence clearly comes first)
- For odd one out, identify the THEME — the odd sentence will talk about something slightly different

---

### DILR — Data Interpretation and Logical Reasoning (40 minutes)

**Section structure:**
- Usually 4-6 sets
- Each set has 4-6 questions
- Total: ~20-24 questions
- Sets can be pure DI (tables, charts, calculations), pure LR (arrangements, puzzles), or mixed

**This is the TRICKIEST section.** One hard set can eat up 15 minutes and give you 0 marks. The key is SET SELECTION.

**Time allocation:**

| Activity | Time | Notes |
|----------|------|-------|
| Read ALL sets (just the intro, not full solving) | 3-5 min | Identify which sets are doable |
| Attempt 3-4 easy/medium sets | ~30-35 min (~8-10 min each) | Focus on sets you CAN solve |
| Review answers | 2-3 min | Check for silly mistakes |

**Strategy:**
1. **First 3-5 minutes: READ EVERY SET.** Don't start solving yet. Just read the introduction of each set and categorize:
   - **Easy:** You can see the approach immediately. The data is straightforward.
   - **Medium:** You can probably solve it, but it will take some work.
   - **Hard:** You have no idea how to approach it, or the data looks very complex.

2. **Attempt sets in order: Easy first, then Medium.** SKIP all Hard sets entirely. Don't even try.

3. **For each set:**
   - Spend first 2-3 minutes understanding the structure
   - Organize the data (make tables, draw diagrams)
   - Solve questions in order
   - If after 5 minutes you haven't figured out the framework, ABANDON the set

4. **Common DILR set types and approaches:**
   - **Tables/Charts:** Look for patterns, calculate row/column totals
   - **Seating arrangements:** Draw the arrangement, use elimination
   - **Scheduling/Matching:** Make a grid, fill in certain information first
   - **Games/Tournaments:** Draw a bracket, track wins/losses
   - **Bar/Pie charts:** Estimate values, don't calculate exact numbers unless needed

**Targets:**
- Attempts: 12-16 questions (3-4 sets)
- Accuracy: 80-85%
- Time per set: 8-10 minutes maximum

**Golden Rules for DILR:**
- If a set takes more than 12 minutes and you haven't cracked it, MOVE ON
- Better to solve 3 sets perfectly than 5 sets partially
- DILR is the section where set selection matters THE MOST
- Draw diagrams, make tables — don't try to solve in your head
- Many DI questions can be answered by estimation — don't waste time on exact calculations if "approximately" is in the question
- TITA questions in DILR have no negative marking — attempt them even with rough estimates

---

### Quant — Quantitative Ability (40 minutes)

**Section structure:**
- Usually 22-26 individual questions (not in sets like DILR)
- Mix of topics: Arithmetic, Algebra, Geometry, Number Systems, Modern Math
- Mix of difficulty: some very easy, some very hard
- TITA questions: ~5-8 questions (no options given, type the answer — NO NEGATIVE MARKING)

**Time allocation:**

| Activity | Time | Notes |
|----------|------|-------|
| Quick scan — mark easy questions | 2 min | Mentally flag the ones you can solve fast |
| Round 1: Solve ALL easy questions | 20 min | Arithmetic, basic algebra, simple geometry |
| Round 2: Attempt medium questions | 15 min | Topics you are decent at |
| Round 3: Review + attempt TITA | 3 min | Review marked answers, attempt any unanswered TITA |

**Strategy:**
1. **First 2 minutes: SCAN.** Quickly scroll through all questions. Mark mentally:
   - **Easy (solve first):** Percentage, ratio-proportion, averages, simple equations, basic geometry
   - **Medium (solve second):** Moderate algebra, P&C problems you know the approach for, coordinate geometry
   - **Hard (probably skip):** Complex geometry proofs, advanced number theory, tough inequalities

2. **Round 1 (20 min): Easy questions.** These are your guaranteed marks. Solve them carefully — no silly mistakes. Each should take 1-2 minutes.

3. **Round 2 (15 min): Medium questions.** These take 2-3 minutes each. If you get stuck after 3 minutes, mark and move on.

4. **Round 3 (3 min): Review.** Check marked answers for silly mistakes. For any TITA questions you haven't answered, make your best educated guess — there is NO negative marking for TITA.

**Targets:**
- Attempts: 14-18 questions
- Accuracy: 80-85%
- Time per easy question: 1-2 minutes
- Time per medium question: 2-3 minutes

**Golden Rules for Quant:**
- TITA questions have NO negative marking — ALWAYS attempt them, even if you have to guess
- Never spend more than 3 minutes on a single question
- If the calculation looks very messy, you are probably using the wrong approach
- Use answer options for MCQs — plug options back into the question to check
- For geometry: DRAW THE FIGURE. Don't try to solve in your head
- Common trap: questions that look easy but have a twist (read carefully!)
- Arithmetic (percentages, ratios, profit-loss, time-speed-distance) is the highest-scoring topic — master it

## 6. The Art of Skipping (MOST UNDERRATED SKILL)

### Why Skipping Is a Skill

Most students think skipping questions means "giving up." WRONG. Skipping is a STRATEGIC DECISION that maximizes your score.

CAT rewards ACCURACY, not attempts. Here is the math:

### The Math of CAT Scoring

- Correct MCQ answer: +3 marks
- Wrong MCQ answer: -1 mark (negative marking)
- TITA correct: +3 marks
- TITA wrong: 0 marks (no negative marking)
- Unattempted: 0 marks

### Scenario Comparison

**Student A: Attempts 55 questions, 60% accuracy**
```
Correct: 55 x 0.60 = 33 questions x 3 marks = 99 marks
Wrong: 55 x 0.40 = 22 questions x (-1) mark = -22 marks
NET SCORE = 99 - 22 = 77 marks
```

**Student B: Attempts 40 questions, 85% accuracy**
```
Correct: 40 x 0.85 = 34 questions x 3 marks = 102 marks
Wrong: 40 x 0.15 = 6 questions x (-1) mark = -6 marks
NET SCORE = 102 - 6 = 96 marks
```

**Student B scores 96 marks vs Student A's 77 marks.** Student B attempted 15 FEWER questions but scored 19 marks MORE.

### Another Example (More Extreme)

**Student C: Attempts 60 questions, 55% accuracy**
```
Correct: 33 x 3 = 99 marks
Wrong: 27 x (-1) = -27 marks
NET SCORE = 72 marks
```

**Student D: Attempts 35 questions, 90% accuracy**
```
Correct: 31.5 (round to 32) x 3 = 96 marks
Wrong: 3 x (-1) = -3 marks
NET SCORE = 93 marks
```

Student D attempted NEARLY HALF the questions but scored 21 marks MORE.

### The Lesson

**FEWER attempts with HIGH accuracy BEATS more attempts with low accuracy.** Every time.

### How to Identify "Skip-Worthy" Questions

Ask yourself these questions when you read a problem:

**SKIP if ANY of these are true:**
1. You read it twice and have NO idea how to even start
2. It requires more than 3 minutes of calculation (you can sense this)
3. It is a topic you are weak in AND it is clearly hard difficulty
4. The passage/set looks confusing even after reading the introduction
5. (For DILR) After 5 minutes, you have not figured out the framework for the set
6. The question has 4-5 conditions that need to be tracked simultaneously
7. It involves a formula or concept you have not revised recently and are unsure about

**ATTEMPT if ANY of these are true:**
1. You know the approach immediately upon reading
2. It is a topic you are strong in
3. It is a TITA question (no negative marking — always worth trying)
4. You can eliminate 2-3 options even if you cannot solve completely
5. It requires simple calculations
6. You have seen a similar question before

### The Skipping Mindset

Think of it this way: every question you skip saves you 2-3 minutes that you can use on another question you CAN solve correctly. Skipping a hard question to spend time on an easier question is not giving up — it is SMART strategy.

Professional exam-takers (people who consistently score 99+ percentile) skip 25-35% of questions in every mock and every exam. They know that attempting everything is a LOSING strategy.

## 7. Mock Analysis — THE MOST IMPORTANT PART

> If you take a mock but don't analyze it, you have wasted 2 hours.

Mock analysis is where the REAL learning happens. The mock itself just shows you where you stand. The analysis is what moves you forward.

**Spend 1-2 hours analyzing EVERY mock.** This is non-negotiable.

---

### Step 1: Score Breakdown (15 minutes)

Immediately after the mock (after your 15-30 minute break), record these numbers:

```
Mock #: ___    Date: ___    Provider: ___

VARC:
  - Attempted: ___/24 (or however many questions)
  - Correct: ___
  - Wrong: ___
  - Score: ___
  - Accuracy: ___% (correct / attempted x 100)

DILR:
  - Attempted: ___/24
  - Correct: ___
  - Wrong: ___
  - Score: ___
  - Accuracy: ___%

Quant:
  - Attempted: ___/26
  - Correct: ___
  - Wrong: ___
  - Score: ___
  - Accuracy: ___%

Overall:
  - Total Score: ___
  - Percentile: ___
  - Time taken per section: ___
```

Put this in your Score Tracker spreadsheet (template in Section 12).

---

### Step 2: Categorize EVERY Question (30-45 minutes)

Go through EVERY question in the mock — not just the ones you got wrong. Categorize each into one of 6 buckets:

| Category | Symbol | What It Means | What To Do |
|----------|--------|---------------|------------|
| Correct + Confident | Green check | You knew the method and got it right | Nothing — this is your strength |
| Correct + Lucky Guess | Yellow check | You guessed or were unsure, but got lucky | LEARN the actual method — next time you might not be lucky |
| Wrong + Silly Mistake | Red X (silly) | You knew how to solve it but made a careless error | Add to your MISTAKE LOG with the error type |
| Wrong + Concept Gap | Red X (concept) | You didn't know the concept or approach | Study that topic — add it to your weak areas list |
| Skipped + Could Have Solved | Grey circle | You skipped it but the solution was within your ability | Time management issue — you need to skip smarter |
| Skipped + Correctly Skipped | Green circle | It was hard and you were right to skip | Good decision — this means your skip sense is working |

**How to do this practically:**
- Open the mock solutions on your screen
- Go through question by question
- For each question, read the solution and honestly decide which category it falls into
- Keep a tally: how many in each category?

**Ideal distribution (for a student scoring ~85-90 percentile):**
- Correct + Confident: 55-65% of attempted
- Correct + Lucky Guess: 5-10% (should be low — means you are guessing too much)
- Wrong + Silly Mistake: 5-10% (target: reduce this to under 5%)
- Wrong + Concept Gap: 5-10% (tells you what to study)
- Skipped + Could Have Solved: Under 5% (tells you to manage time better)
- Skipped + Correctly Skipped: 20-30% (shows good set/question selection)

---

### Step 3: Error Analysis (20-30 minutes)

For every question you got WRONG, identify the TYPE of mistake:

| Mistake Type | Description | Example |
|--------------|-------------|---------|
| Calculation Error | Wrong arithmetic, missed a step | Added instead of subtracted, 7x8=54 |
| Misread Question | Missed a keyword in the question | Missed "not", "except", "approximately", "at least" |
| Concept Error | Used wrong formula or approach | Applied simple interest formula for compound interest |
| Time Pressure Error | Knew the method but rushed | Skipped a step to save time, got wrong answer |
| Trap Error | Fell for a deliberately misleading option | Chose the "obvious" answer that was a trap |
| Careless Error | Filled wrong option, copied wrong number | Calculated 42 but marked option B (which was 24) |
| Reading Error (RC) | Misunderstood the passage or question intent | Chose "author agrees" when question asked "author disagrees" |

**Track these over time.** After 5-10 mocks, you will see clear patterns:
- "I make calculation errors in Quant 40% of the time"
- "I misread VARC questions 30% of the time"
- "I fall for traps in DILR 20% of the time"

These patterns tell you EXACTLY what to fix.

---

### Step 4: Create and Maintain Your Mistake Log (Ongoing)

This is your MOST VALUABLE document. For each mistake, record:

```
Mock #: 12    Question #: Q47    Section: Quant
Topic: Percentages
Mistake Type: Misread Question
What happened: Question asked "percentage DECREASE" but I calculated "percentage CHANGE" and gave a positive answer
Correct approach: Read the question twice. "Decrease" means answer should be negative/less than original.
Lesson: Always underline keywords like increase/decrease/at least/at most
```

**Before EVERY mock, re-read your last 10 entries in the Mistake Log.** This trains your brain to watch for those specific errors.

You will be amazed — after 10 mocks, your most common mistake type will drop by 50% just because you are AWARE of it.

---

### Step 5: Weak Area Identification (15 minutes)

After every 5 mocks, do a deeper analysis:

1. **Topic-wise accuracy:** Which Quant topics are you consistently getting wrong?
   - Percentage, Ratio, Profit-Loss: ___% accuracy
   - Algebra: ___% accuracy
   - Geometry: ___% accuracy
   - Number Systems: ___% accuracy
   - P&C, Probability: ___% accuracy
   - Time-Speed-Distance, Time-Work: ___% accuracy

2. **Section-wise trend:** Is your VARC improving? Is DILR stuck?

3. **Accuracy trend:** Is your accuracy going up or down?

4. **Time management:** Are you consistently running out of time in one section?

5. **Weak areas action plan:**
   For each weak area: What will you study this week to fix it?

   Example:
   ```
   Weak area: Geometry (45% accuracy over last 5 mocks)
   Action: Spend 1 hour on Tuesday + Thursday revising geometry formulas
   Specific topics: Circle theorems, triangle similarity, coordinate geometry
   Resources: Arun Sharma Chapter 14-16, Cracku geometry videos
   Target: 65% accuracy in next 5 mocks
   ```

## 8. Score Improvement Timeline

Your scores will NOT improve in a straight line. Here is what to expect:

### Mocks 1-5: Understanding Phase
- **What happens:** You are learning the exam format, getting used to timing, building basic stamina
- **Scores:** Likely low and all over the place
- **Don't worry about:** Exact scores or percentiles
- **Focus on:** Completing the full mock, starting mock analysis habit

### Mocks 6-10: Time Management Phase
- **What happens:** You are getting better at managing 40 minutes per section, learning when to skip
- **Scores:** Slowly improving, still inconsistent
- **Don't worry about:** Score fluctuations (one good mock followed by a bad one is normal)
- **Focus on:** Accuracy percentage (aim for 70%+), reducing silly mistakes

### Mocks 11-15: The Plateau (THIS IS NORMAL)
- **What happens:** Your scores will STOP improving. You might even score LOWER than mock #10.
- **Why:** Your easy improvements are done. Now you need to fix deeper issues.
- **Scores:** Flat or slightly declining
- **Don't panic:** THIS HAPPENS TO EVERYONE. The plateau is where most students give up. DON'T.
- **Focus on:** Deep mock analysis, fixing mistake patterns, strengthening weak topics

### Mocks 16-20: Breakthrough Phase
- **What happens:** Your weak area fixes start showing results. Scores jump.
- **Scores:** Noticeable improvement (5-10 percentile jump)
- **Why now:** Your brain has seen enough patterns, your mistake patterns are reducing, weak areas are getting stronger
- **Focus on:** Consistency (every mock within a 10-percentile range)

### Mocks 20-30: Fine-Tuning Phase
- **What happens:** You are now optimizing, not learning. Small adjustments lead to small but important gains.
- **Scores:** Stable with gradual improvement
- **Focus on:** Exam-day strategy, building confidence, maintaining stamina
- **Target:** Consistent scores within your target range

### Mocks 30+: Confidence Phase
- **What happens:** You know the exam inside out. No surprises.
- **Scores:** Consistent and near your peak
- **Focus on:** Staying sharp, not getting complacent, maintaining routine

### Key Reminder About Score Fluctuations

Even at 30+ mocks, you WILL have bad days. A 90-percentile student will occasionally score 75 percentile. This is NORMAL. Do not panic. Analyze why (hard paper? low stamina that day? skipped analysis last week?) and move on.

The goal is CONSISTENCY, not perfection.

## 9. Last Month Strategy (30 Days Before CAT)

The last month is NOT the time for new learning. It is the time for SHARPENING what you already know.

### Weekly Schedule (Last Month)

| Day | Activity |
|-----|----------|
| Monday | Revise top 3 weak topics + review mistake log |
| Tuesday | Practice problems from weak areas (1.5 hours) |
| Wednesday | FULL MOCK #1 (morning) + Analysis (afternoon) |
| Thursday | Fix errors from Wednesday mock + revise formulas |
| Friday | Light practice + review mistake log + REST |
| Saturday | FULL MOCK #2 (morning) + Analysis (afternoon) |
| Sunday | Complete rest — NO STUDYING. Let your brain recover. |

### What To Do

1. **Take 2 full mocks per week** — Saturday and Wednesday, both under exam conditions
2. **Analyze each mock thoroughly** — Follow the full 5-step analysis process
3. **Revise only what you know** — NO new topics. If you haven't learned Permutation & Combination by now, it is too late. Focus on the 15-18 topics you ARE good at.
4. **Review your mistake log daily** — 10 minutes every morning. Read through your last 20 entries.
5. **Revise formulas and shortcuts** — Make a formula sheet and review it 3 times a week
6. **Solve previous year CAT papers** — These are your best practice at this stage

### What NOT To Do

1. **Don't start new topics** — You won't learn them well enough in a month
2. **Don't study more than 4-5 hours a day** — Burnout is real and will hurt your exam-day performance
3. **Don't take more than 2 mocks per week** — Diminishing returns + exhaustion
4. **Don't study the night before the exam** — Complete rest
5. **Don't compare your scores with friends** — Different mock providers have different difficulty levels. Your 80 percentile on IMS might equal their 90 percentile on a different platform.

### Physical Health = Mental Performance

This is NOT touchy-feely advice. Your brain is a physical organ. It runs on sleep, food, and oxygen.

- **Sleep 7-8 hours every night** — Sleep deprivation reduces cognitive performance by 20-30%
- **Exercise 30 minutes daily** — Even a brisk walk. Exercise improves focus and reduces anxiety
- **Eat well** — Avoid heavy meals before mocks. Light, nutritious food keeps your brain sharp.
- **Stay hydrated** — Dehydration causes headaches and reduces concentration
- **Limit screen time** — Outside of study and mocks, reduce phone/social media usage. Your brain needs rest.

### The Day Before CAT

- Light revision only (formula sheet, mistake log)
- No mocks
- No heavy studying
- Go for a walk or light exercise
- Eat a normal dinner (not too heavy, not too light)
- Set 2 alarms for the morning
- Pack everything you need: admit card, ID proof, pen, water bottle
- Sleep by 10 PM

## 10. Exam Day Strategy

### Morning Routine

1. Wake up early (at least 2 hours before you need to leave)
2. Light breakfast — dosa/idli/oats/toast (not heavy paratha or biryani)
3. Review your formula sheet ONE last time (10 minutes max)
4. Read your top 5 entries from your mistake log (5 minutes)
5. Leave home with plenty of time — reach the exam center 1 hour early

### At the Exam Center

- **Don't discuss topics with other students** — This only increases anxiety. If someone says "Did you prepare XYZ topic? It's very important!" — ignore them. You have prepared what you have prepared.
- **Use the bathroom** before entering the exam hall
- **Carry your admit card and valid photo ID** — check these the night before
- **Bring a water bottle** (transparent, without label — as per CAT rules)
- **Take a few deep breaths** before the exam starts. You have done this 30+ times in mocks. This is just another mock.

### During the Exam — Section Strategy

**First 1 minute: Read on-screen instructions carefully.**
Even though you know the format from mocks, read them. Sometimes there are small changes.

**Start with your STRONGEST section** (if CAT allows section order choice — check the year's rules). Starting with your best section builds confidence and momentum. If section order is fixed, that is fine — just follow it.

**For EACH section, follow this approach:**

| Time | Action |
|------|--------|
| Minutes 1-3 | SCAN all questions. Mentally mark Easy/Medium/Hard |
| Minutes 3-25 | Solve EASY questions first (Round 1) |
| Minutes 25-37 | Solve MEDIUM questions (Round 2) |
| Minutes 37-40 | Review marked answers. NO new hard questions. |

**Special exam-day rules:**
- **TITA questions: ALWAYS attempt** — Zero negative marking. Even an educated guess is worth it.
- **Last 3 minutes: No new questions.** Only review what you have already answered. Check for silly mistakes — did you mark the right option?
- **Don't change answers unless 100% sure.** Research shows that your first instinct is right 65-70% of the time. Changing answers usually makes things WORSE, not better.
- **If you get stuck on a question, MOVE ON.** You can always come back within the section if you have time.

### Managing Exam-Day Anxiety

- **Physical trick:** Press your feet firmly into the floor. This grounds you physically and reduces anxiety.
- **Breathing trick:** Breathe in for 4 counts, hold for 4 counts, breathe out for 4 counts. Do this 3 times.
- **Mindset trick:** Remind yourself: "I have done 30+ mocks. I know this exam. This is just another practice session."
- **If a section goes badly:** FORGET about it. You CANNOT change it. The next section is a fresh start. Many 99-percentilers have had one bad section — they made up for it in the other two.

### After the Exam

- Don't discuss answers with friends immediately — this causes unnecessary stress
- If you want to check, wait for official answer keys (takes a few days)
- Reward yourself — you worked hard. Take a day off.

## 11. Common Mistakes in CAT Preparation (Avoid ALL of These)

### Mistake 1: "I'll start mocks after finishing the syllabus"
**Why it's wrong:** You will NEVER feel 100% ready. The syllabus is too vast. Start mocks with whatever you know — mocks will tell you what else to study.
**What to do instead:** Start mocks 3 months before CAT, even if you have only covered 50% of syllabus.

### Mistake 2: Taking mocks but not analyzing them
**Why it's wrong:** A mock without analysis is just 2 hours of stress. The learning happens in the analysis, not the mock itself.
**What to do instead:** Spend 1-2 hours analyzing every mock. Follow the 5-step process in Section 7.

### Mistake 3: Attempting too many questions
**Why it's wrong:** CAT has negative marking. Low accuracy destroys your score even if you attempt everything.
**What to do instead:** Aim for 40-45 attempts with 80-85% accuracy. Quality over quantity.

### Mistake 4: Spending too long on one question
**Why it's wrong:** Every minute you spend on a hard question is a minute taken away from an easy question you could have solved.
**What to do instead:** Set a strict 3-minute limit per question. If you haven't cracked it by then, skip and move on.

### Mistake 5: Not simulating real exam conditions
**Why it's wrong:** Your mock score is meaningless if you took it on your phone, in bed, with WhatsApp open.
**What to do instead:** Follow the full pre-mock setup checklist in Section 4. Make every mock as real as possible.

### Mistake 6: Ignoring TITA questions
**Why it's wrong:** TITA questions have ZERO negative marking. Not attempting them is leaving free marks on the table.
**What to do instead:** ALWAYS attempt TITA questions. Even if you are unsure, give your best estimate.

### Mistake 7: Not tracking mistakes over time
**Why it's wrong:** You will keep making the same errors if you don't track and fix them.
**What to do instead:** Maintain a Mistake Log (see Section 7, Step 4). Review it before every mock.

### Mistake 8: Studying the night before the exam
**Why it's wrong:** Your brain needs rest to perform. Last-minute cramming increases anxiety and reduces focus.
**What to do instead:** Light formula review (10 min) + early dinner + good sleep (7-8 hours).

### Mistake 9: Comparing scores with friends
**Why it's wrong:** Different mock providers have very different difficulty levels. Your 75 percentile on IMS might be harder than your friend's 85 percentile on another platform.
**What to do instead:** Compare only with YOUR past scores, not with others. Track your own trend.

### Mistake 10: Giving up after a bad mock
**Why it's wrong:** EVERYONE has bad mocks. Even 99-percentilers score poorly sometimes. One bad mock means nothing.
**What to do instead:** Analyze the bad mock carefully, find what went wrong, fix it, and take the next mock. The trend matters, not individual scores.

### Mistake 11: Solving too many questions from books but not from mocks
**Why it's wrong:** Book questions are untimed, unpressured, and usually sorted by topic. The exam is timed, pressured, and mixed.
**What to do instead:** Ratio should be: 60% mock/past-paper practice + 40% topic-wise book practice.

### Mistake 12: Not working on speed
**Why it's wrong:** Many students can solve questions correctly given unlimited time. CAT gives you 40 minutes per section. Speed matters.
**What to do instead:** Practice with a timer ALWAYS. Set a 2-minute target for easy questions. Use shortcuts and mental math.

### Mistake 13: Ignoring VARC because "you can't improve English"
**Why it's wrong:** VARC is 33% of your score. RC skills CAN be improved with practice. Reading speed and comprehension improve with consistent effort.
**What to do instead:** Read 1 editorial daily (The Hindu, Livemint, Economic Times). Practice 1-2 RC passages daily. VARC is the most improvable section with consistent reading.

### Mistake 14: Spending too much time on theory and not enough on application
**Why it's wrong:** Knowing a formula is not the same as knowing WHEN and HOW to apply it under time pressure.
**What to do instead:** For every 30 minutes of theory, do 30 minutes of practice problems.

### Mistake 15: Not sleeping enough
**Why it's wrong:** Sleep deprivation reduces problem-solving ability, reading comprehension, and mathematical accuracy by 20-30%.
**What to do instead:** 7-8 hours of sleep, non-negotiable. Sleep more during the last month, not less.
