## Why DSA Matters for AI/ML Engineering Interviews

Here's the honest truth that nobody tells beginners:

> **Even AI/ML engineering roles at big tech make you pass the same DSA coding interview as a regular software engineer.**

Microsoft, Google, and most top companies use a **"raise the bar" coding round**. Before they even discuss your ML knowledge, they want to see:

1. **Can you solve a problem you've never seen before?** (problem-solving)
2. **Can you write clean, correct, bug-free code under pressure?** (engineering)
3. **Can you reason about efficiency?** (Big-O — "will this scale to a billion users?")
4. **Can you communicate while thinking?** (collaboration)

Think of it this way:

| What ML knowledge proves | What DSA round proves |
|---|---|
| You understand models | You can build the *systems* that serve those models |
| You can use libraries | You can write efficient code from scratch |
| You know the theory | You can debug live, under pressure |

ML engineers write data pipelines, optimize training loops, and serve models to millions. **All of that is DSA in disguise.** A slow algorithm in a training loop can cost thousands of dollars and hours of GPU time. Big tech knows this — that's why they test it.

**Bottom line:** DSA is the toll booth on the highway to a big-tech AI job. This module gets you through it.

## Your LeetCode Practice Plan (150–300 problems)

You don't need to solve all 3,000+ LeetCode problems. **Quality and patterns beat quantity.** Here's a realistic, proven plan.

### The Philosophy: Learn Patterns, Not Problems

There are roughly **15 core patterns** that cover 90% of interview questions. Once you recognize "oh, this is a sliding window problem," you already know the approach. This module teaches you those patterns.

### Recommended Volume by Goal

| Your situation | Target problems | Timeline |
|---|---|---|
| Have 2+ months | **150 problems** | ~2-3 per day |
| Have 4-6 months (recommended) | **200 problems** | ~1-2 per day, deeper study |
| Want to be very confident | **300 problems** | spread over 6 months |

> **Sheetal's recommended path: aim for 200 problems over ~4 months.** That's a sustainable ~12-15/week, with rest days.

### Difficulty Mix

```
Easy   ████████████░░░░░░░░  ~30%  (build confidence, learn syntax)
Medium ██████████████████░░  ~60%  (THE interview level — focus here)
Hard   ████░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░  ~10%  (stretch goals, top companies)
```

**Most big-tech interview questions are Medium.** Don't over-index on Hard problems — they're rare and demoralizing early on.

### The Curated Lists (use these, don't wander randomly)

1. **NeetCode 150** (neetcode.io) — the gold standard. Organized by the exact patterns this module teaches. *Start here.*
2. **Blind 75** — a famous 75-problem subset. The absolute must-knows. (NeetCode 150 includes these.)
3. **LeetCode "Top Interview 150"** — LeetCode's official curated list.
4. **Grind 75** (techinterviewhandbook.org/grind75) — lets you set a weekly schedule.

### Suggested Weekly Rhythm

```
Mon-Fri:  2 problems/day  (1 review of an old one + 1 new)
Saturday: 3-4 problems (one slightly harder, simulate timing)
Sunday:   REST or review your "mistakes notebook"
```

### Keep a "Mistakes Notebook"

Every time you fail a problem or peek at the solution, write down:
- The problem name + pattern
- *Why* you got stuck (the key insight you missed)
- The one-line trick

Review this weekly. **This is the single highest-leverage habit** for interview prep.
