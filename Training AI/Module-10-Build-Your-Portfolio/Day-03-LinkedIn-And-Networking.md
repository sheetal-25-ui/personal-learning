# Module 10 — Day 3: LinkedIn and Networking

## The Uncomfortable Truth About Getting Hired

Most jobs — especially good ones — are filled through **people, not job boards.** Studies consistently show that a large share of hires come through referrals. A referral can move your resume from "200 in the pile" to "the recruiter actually reads it today."

So networking isn't optional schmoozing. It's the highest-leverage thing you can do. And the good news: **you don't have to be charming or extroverted.** You just have to be visible, helpful, and consistent.

Today we set up your LinkedIn so recruiters find *you*, and give you copy-paste templates to reach out to *them*.

## Part 1: Optimize Your LinkedIn Profile

Recruiters search LinkedIn with keywords like "machine learning engineer PyTorch." Your job is to **show up in those searches** and look credible when you do.

### The Profile Checklist

| Element | What to do | Why |
|---------|-----------|-----|
| **Photo** | Clear, friendly, professional headshot | Profiles with photos get far more views |
| **Headline** | Keyword-rich, not just "Student" | This is what shows in search results |
| **About** | 3–4 short paragraphs, first person | Your story + skills + what you want |
| **Experience** | Mirror your resume bullets | Consistency = credibility |
| **Projects/Featured** | Pin your GitHub projects | Visual proof of skills |
| **Skills** | Add 10–15 relevant skills | Recruiters filter by these |
| **Open to Work** | Turn it on | Signals recruiters you're available |
| **Custom URL** | linkedin.com/in/sheetal-tyagi | Clean link for your resume |

### Your Headline (Don't Waste This)
Your headline is the most-read line on your profile. Pack it with keywords.

❌ Weak: `Student | Looking for opportunities`
✅ Strong: `Aspiring AI/ML Engineer | Python, PyTorch, LLMs & RAG | Building ML projects | Open to opportunities`

> Include the exact job title you want ("AI/ML Engineer") and your top tools. These are the words recruiters type into search.

### Use the "Featured" and "Projects" Sections
- **Featured:** Pin links to your top 2–3 GitHub repos (with screenshots — LinkedIn shows a preview image).
- **Projects:** Add each Module 8 project with a description and GitHub link.

> This is where Day 01 (GitHub) and LinkedIn connect. Your repos become visual proof right on your profile.

### Turn On "Open to Work"
Settings → "Open to work" → choose **"Recruiters only"** (a subtle green ring) or the public **#OpenToWork** banner. Recruiters specifically filter for this.

## Part 2: Build in Public

"Building in public" means **sharing your learning journey openly**. It feels awkward at first, but it's the single best way to build a network without "networking."

### Why it works
- It creates a paper trail of your growth that recruiters can see.
- It attracts people *to you* — opportunities, advice, connections.
- It compounds. Post 1 gets 5 likes; post 20 might get you a DM from a recruiter.

### What to post (you have endless material from this course)
| Post type | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| **Project announcement** | "Just shipped my RAG app — chat with your own PDFs! Built with embeddings + Claude API. Here's what I learned 🧵" |
| **Lesson learned** | "Spent 3 hours debugging why my CNN overfit. Turns out I forgot data augmentation. Here's the fix:" |
| **Milestone** | "Finished my deep learning module. Built a 94%-accuracy image classifier in PyTorch. On to transformers!" |
| **Resource share** | "This explanation of how attention works finally made it click for me:" |

### A simple posting formula
1. **Hook** (1 line that makes people stop scrolling)
2. **What you did / learned** (2–4 lines)
3. **A takeaway or screenshot**
4. **A light call to action** ("What would you add?" / link to the repo)

> **Consistency beats brilliance.** One post a week for 3 months will change your network. Don't aim for viral — aim for steady and genuine.

## Part 3: Reaching Out to People

This is where most people freeze. Here's the mindset shift: **you're not begging — you're being curious and helpful.** Most people genuinely like helping someone earlier in their journey.

### The Rules of Good Outreach
1. **Personalize every message.** Generic "Hi, please refer me" gets ignored.
2. **Lead with genuine interest**, not a request.
3. **Keep it short.** Respect their time.
4. **Make the "ask" tiny and specific** (a 15-min call, one question — not "get me a job").
5. **Connection requests have a note limit (~200–300 characters)** — be concise.

### Template 1: Connecting with someone at a target company
```
Hi [Name], I came across your work on [team/project] at [Anthropic/Microsoft].
I'm an aspiring ML engineer building LLM and RAG projects, and I really admire
what your team is doing. Would love to connect and follow along!
```

### Template 2: Informational chat request (after connecting)
```
Hi [Name], thanks for connecting! I'm working toward an AI/ML engineer role and
you've built exactly the kind of career I'm aiming for. Would you be open to a
quick 15-minute call sometime? I'd love to hear how you got into [their area] and
any advice for someone breaking in. Totally understand if you're swamped.
```

### Template 3: Asking about a referral (only after some rapport)
```
Hi [Name], I saw [Company] is hiring for an ML Engineer (req #[number]) and it
looks like a great fit for my background in [PyTorch / LLM apps]. I've attached my
resume and a few of my projects. If you feel comfortable referring me, I'd be
truly grateful — and no worries at all if not. Thanks for considering it!
```

### Template 4: Thanking + staying in touch (always do this)
```
Thank you so much for the chat / the referral, [Name] — it genuinely helped.
I'll keep you posted on how it goes. If there's ever anything I can help YOU with,
just say the word.
```

> **Etiquette:** Don't ask for a referral the moment you connect. Build a tiny bit of rapport first — a comment on their post, a thoughtful question. And always follow up with a thank-you. People remember gratitude.

### The numbers game (and how to stay sane)
- Many people won't reply. **That's normal — it's not personal.**
- Send a handful of thoughtful messages per week, not 100 spammy ones.
- Track who you contacted and when (a simple spreadsheet works).

## Part 4: Finding Referrals

A referral is the cheat code. Here's how to find people who can give you one:

1. **Search the company on LinkedIn** → "People" tab → filter by title ("ML Engineer," "Recruiter").
2. **Look for second-degree connections** — people who know someone you know. Ask the mutual connection for a warm intro.
3. **Prioritize alumni** from your school or past company — shared background = higher reply rate.
4. **Engage before you ask** — like/comment on their posts for a couple weeks so your name is familiar.
5. **Use referral programs** — many companies (Microsoft, Google) have formal referral systems; a connection just needs to submit you.

> A warm referral from an alum often beats a cold application by a mile. Start with people who already have a reason to help you.

## Part 5: Communities to Join

Communities give you peers, answers, job leads, and people to network with — all in one place.

| Community | Where | Good for |
|-----------|-------|----------|
| **r/MachineLearning, r/learnmachinelearning** | Reddit | Learning, news, project feedback |
| **Hugging Face forums & Discord** | huggingface.co | LLMs, models, very active |
| **Kaggle** | kaggle.com | Competitions, datasets, a *profile that counts as a portfolio* |
| **MLOps / AI Discord servers** | Discord | Real-time help, job channels |
| **Local AI/ML meetups** | Meetup, lu.ma | In-person networking, referrals |
| **X / Twitter ML community** | x.com | Follow researchers, build-in-public |
| **Anthropic / OpenAI Discord & forums** | official sites | News from your target companies |

> **Kaggle bonus:** Competing on Kaggle (even just bronze-tier finishes) gives you a public, verifiable record of ML skill — and great resume material. Treat it as both learning and portfolio-building.
